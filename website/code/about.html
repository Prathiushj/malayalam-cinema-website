Legendary Malayalam Films

Chemmeen(1965)

Directed by Ramu Kariat, Often considered the first "masterpiece" of Malayalam cinema, Chemmeen was based on the novel by T. S. Pillai and set in a coastal village. It marked the beginning of serious, sophisticated cinema in Kerala and was the first Malayalam film to win the National Film Award for Best Feature Film.

Plot Summary

The film revolves around the tragic love story between Karuthamma (played by Sheela), the daughter of a Hindu fisherman, and Pareekutty (played by Madhu), a Muslim trader. Set against the backdrop of the coastal fishing community in Kerala, it explores the themes of love, betrayal, social taboos, and the clash between tradition and modern desires. The central conflict stems from the deep-rooted belief that the sea goddess, Kadalamma, will curse those who betray the sanctity of marriage, leading to disastrous consequences for the community. The narrative delicately intertwines romance with the cultural and spiritual beliefs of the coastal folk, making it a story rich with emotional depth.

Cinematic Brilliance

"Chemmeen" is celebrated for its technical achievements and visual storytelling. The stunning cinematography by Marcus Bartley captures the scenic beauty of Kerala’s coastlines, bringing the vividness of the fishing community to life. The film's use of natural light, expansive shots of the sea, and artistic compositions make it a visual treat.

Iconic Music and Soundtrack

One of the film’s greatest strengths is its memorable soundtrack, composed by Salil Chowdhury with lyrics by Vayalar Ramavarma. The songs, such as "Manasa Maine Varu" and "Pennale Pennale", are timeless classics in Malayalam music. The haunting melodies, coupled with evocative lyrics, enhance the film’s emotional atmosphere and have remained beloved among fans for decades.

Cultural Impact and Legacy

"Chemmeen" is more than just a love story; it is a social commentary on the rigid caste and religious barriers in traditional Kerala society. The film’s exploration of themes like superstition, faith, and social ostracism made it a bold narrative for its time. It challenged societal norms while remaining rooted in the cultural ethos of Kerala, making it both a commercial success and a critical darling.

The film’s success also helped bring Malayalam cinema to the national spotlight. By winning the National Award for Best Feature Film, it opened doors for future Malayalam films to be recognized on a national and international scale.

Nearly six decades after its release, "Chemmeen" continues to be a timeless classic that resonates with audiences. It is a masterpiece that reflects the power of cinema to tell stories that are both culturally specific and universally relatable. The film stands as a testament to the rich storytelling traditions of Kerala and remains an essential watch for anyone interested in exploring the roots of Indian cinema.

Whether you're a film enthusiast or simply someone exploring classic Indian films, "Chemmeen" remains a must-watch for its poignant narrative, brilliant performances, and unforgettable music.

Manichitrathazhu(1993)

Directed by Fazil, Manichitrathazhu became a landmark in the Malayalam film industry due to its innovative blend of psychological thriller, family drama, and horror. The film revolutionized the way suspense and thriller genres were approached in Indian cinema.

Plot Summary

Set in a sprawling, ancestral mansion, the story revolves around Ganga (played by Shobana), a modern, educated woman who visits her husband's ancestral home. The mansion is believed to be haunted by the spirit of Nagavalli, a dancer who was wronged in the past. Despite warnings, Ganga and her husband, Nakulan (Suresh Gopi), decide to stay there. Soon, strange events begin to unfold, and Ganga's behavior changes, leading to chaos among the family members.

To resolve the mysterious happenings, Nakulan invites his psychiatrist friend, Dr. Sunny Joseph (played by Mohanlal). What follows is a brilliant narrative where Dr. Sunny delves into unraveling the psychological complexities behind the events, exposing the thin line between superstition and reality.

Iconic Characters and Perfomances

1. Mohanlal as Dr. Sunny Joseph: Mohanlal's portrayal of the laid-back yet sharp psychiatrist is legendary. His effortless blend of humor, wit, and intellect brings a unique charm to the character, making him one of the most memorable figures in Malayalam cinema.

2. Shobana as Ganga/Nagavalli: Shobana delivered an outstanding performance, portraying the dual personalities of Ganga and Nagavalli with remarkable depth. Her portrayal of the character’s transformation earned her the National Film Award for Best Actress.

3. Suresh Gopi as Nakulan: Playing the concerned husband who struggles to understand the changes in his wife, Suresh Gopi added emotional weight to the film.

Themes and Cultural Impact

"Manichitrathazhu" is not just a thriller; it delves deep into the psyche, exploring themes of mental health, superstition, and the clash between tradition and modernity. The film skillfully weaves together psychological drama with supernatural elements, keeping audiences on the edge of their seats while delivering a profound message about the dangers of misinterpreting mental illnesses as paranormal phenomena.

The film is notable for its authentic portrayal of Kerala’s heritage, showcasing traditional architecture, customs, and art forms, which enrich the visual and cultural depth of the narrative.

Memorable Music and Soundtrack

The haunting background score and songs composed by M.G. Radhakrishnan add to the film’s eerie atmosphere. Songs like "Oru Murai Vanthu Parthaya" have achieved cult status and are still cherished by music lovers for their haunting melodies and evocative lyrics.

Technical Mastery

The film’s cinematography by Anandakkuttan and the tight editing by T.R. Shekhar create an immersive atmosphere, capturing the grandeur and mystery of the mansion while enhancing the suspense. The brilliant use of lighting and shadows amplifies the film’s eerie mood, making it a visual treat.

Drishyam(2013)

Directed by Jeethu Joseph, Drishyam is one of the most significant films in the history of Malayalam cinema, not only because of its commercial success but also due to its groundbreaking narrative and ability to captivate audiences both in India and internationally.The film stands as a milestone in the evolution of modern Malayalam cinema, blending elements of suspense, drama, and thriller in a way that had never been done before in the industry.

Plot Summary

Set in the picturesque village of Pachalam, the film centers around Georgekutty (played by Mohanlal), an ordinary man who leads a simple life with his wife Rani (played by Meena) and two daughters. Georgekutty, who runs a small cable TV service, is an intelligent, resourceful man with a deep love for cinema.

The plot takes a dramatic turn when Georgekutty's family becomes entangled in a crime that is unintentionally committed by his daughter. The crime leads to an investigation by the local police, headed by IG Geetha Prabhakar (played by Asha Sharath), who is determined to uncover the truth. Faced with mounting pressure and the potential destruction of his family, Georgekutty uses his wit, understanding of human behavior, and knowledge of cinema to devise a plan to protect his loved ones.

What follows is a taut, gripping narrative that keeps the audience on edge as Georgekutty navigates through the maze of investigation and potential exposure, all while maintaining his family's innocence.

Iconic Characters and Perfomances

1. Mohanlal as Georgekutty: The heart of the film is Mohanlal’s outstanding performance as Georgekutty. His portrayal of a calm, strategic, and emotionally driven family man is widely regarded as one of his best. With his subtle expressions and understated brilliance, Mohanlal brings depth and complexity to the character, making Georgekutty one of the most memorable characters in Malayalam cinema.

2. Meena as Rani: Meena plays Georgekutty’s wife, Rani, who is initially unaware of the darker turns in the family's life. Meena’s portrayal of a mother and wife caught between fear, guilt, and love is deeply moving and plays a crucial role in the emotional depth of the film.

3. Asha Sharath as IG Geetha: Asha Sharath’s portrayal of the sharp and determined police officer adds a layer of tension to the film. Her character’s investigation into the crime contrasts sharply with Georgekutty’s calculated moves, and their interactions drive much of the film's suspense.

Themes and Cultural Impact

"Drishyam" is a masterclass in storytelling. At its core, the film is about family, survival, and morality. It explores the lengths to which an ordinary person would go to protect their loved ones and the moral dilemmas faced in the process. The film's exploration of human emotions, loyalty, and justice resonates with audiences on a universal level, transcending regional boundaries.

The film also tackles themes of social pressure, power dynamics, and the flaws in the justice system. Georgekutty’s decision-making and actions question the morality of the legal system and highlight the complexities of right and wrong, forcing viewers to think about the consequences of their actions.

"Drishyam" also sparked a discussion about the role of cinema in shaping our understanding of crime and investigation. Georgekutty’s reliance on films and their techniques to outsmart the police plays into the idea of how popular culture influences our thinking and behavior.

Cinematic Brilliance

The film’s direction by Jeethu Joseph is outstanding, creating an atmosphere of tension and suspense without relying on gimmicks or melodrama. The pacing is tight, and every scene is carefully crafted to heighten the suspense, leading to a brilliant and unpredictable climax.

The cinematography by S. Kumar enhances the film’s dark and intense mood. The use of natural light, the portrayal of rural Kerala, and the intimate framing of characters add authenticity and depth to the storytelling.

History of Malayalam Cinema

Founded in the 1920s, Malayalam cinema has seen a rich evolution with legendary filmmakers and a unique style of storytelling. From early black-and-white classics to the contemporary wave of new-age cinema, Malayalam films have earned critical acclaim worldwide.

Early Beginnings (1900s - 1930s)

1. 1913: The first Malayalam feature film, "Vigathakumaran", was directed by J.C. Daniel. However, it is considered a lost film as no copies of the original exist today.

2. 1928: The first Malayalam silent film, "Balan", was produced by Sundar Rao. The film’s release is often considered a significant early milestone in the state's film history.

The Birth of Sound and Color (1930s - 1950s)

1. 1930s: The Malayalam film industry began to gain traction with the introduction of sound films. Early sound films included "Janmabhumi" (1939), which was a key film in the transition from silent films to talkies.

2. 1947: The first full-length color film in Malayalam, "Vellinakshatram", was released.

3. 1950s: The post-independence period saw a surge in the number of Malayalam films, although many still followed the traditions of mythological, historical, and melodramatic narratives. Films like "Nritashala" (1950) and "Rama Rajya" (1955) were notable during this time.

The Golden Age (1960s - 1970s)

1. 1961: "Kaliyarangath" (also called "Thiruvonam") is released. This period marked the beginning of more commercially successful and quality productions in Malayalam cinema.

2. 1960s-70s: This era is considered the Golden Age of Malayalam cinema due to the rise of filmmakers like Ramu Kariat, P. Subramaniam, Adoor Gopalakrishnan, and G. Aravindan. "Chemmeen" (1965), directed by Ramu Kariat, became a landmark film and won the National Film Award for Best Feature Film.

3. 1970s: Malayalam cinema witnessed the emergence of art films and films with strong social themes, especially those dealing with the lives of the common people. Directors like G. Aravindan and Adoor Gopalakrishnan pioneered this wave, with films like "Swayamvaram" (1972) and "Thampu" (1978).

New Wave Cinema (1980s - 1990s)

1. 1980s: This period saw the rise of superstars in the industry, most notably Mohanlal and Mammootty. They began to dominate the box office, and this era witnessed the rise of commercial films, particularly the action genre.

2. 1983: "Guru" (directed by Rajiv Anchal) marked a turning point in the way films were made, introducing a more modern, experimental style.

3. 1990s: The 1990s were pivotal in the evolution of Malayalam cinema, with the development of new wave cinema that embraced a blend of realism, social critique, and commercial success. "Manichitrathazhu" (1993), "Meesa Madhavan" (2002), and "Drishyam" (2013) helped shape the new trends, blending thriller, horror, and drama with deeper psychological and emotional insights.

Modern Malayalam Cinema (2000s - Present)

1. 2000s: The early 2000s saw a shift towards realistic cinema, which focused on themes relevant to contemporary life in Kerala. Films like "Kalyanaraman" (2002), "Rajamanikyam" (2005), and "Ustad Hotel" (2012) brought in new themes, narratives, and storytelling techniques.

2. 2010s: The Malayalam film industry continued to evolve, embracing diverse genres such as psychological thrillers ("Drishyam" 2013, "Bangalore Days" 2014), dark comedy ("Kalyana Samayal Saadham" 2013), and biographical films. This period saw the rise of young filmmakers like Lijo Jose Pellissery (known for "Angamaly Diaries", 2017), Dileesh Pothan (known for "Maheshinte Prathikaaram", 2016), and Sidharth Siva.

3. 2013: The release of "Drishyam" was a game-changer, not only for Malayalam cinema but also for Indian cinema as a whole. The film's suspenseful narrative and strong performances by Mohanlal revolutionized the thriller genre.

4. 2020s: Malayalam cinema has continued to garner attention globally. Films such as "The Great Indian Kitchen" (2021), "Home" (2021), and "Joji" (2021) explore contemporary social issues and showcase the industry's versatility, with themes ranging from gender roles to family dynamics and crime.

Key Milestones & Trends

1. Star Power: The 1980s and 90s saw the rise of Malayalam superstars such as Mohanlal and Mammootty, who became the face of mainstream Malayalam cinema. Their popularity has continued to shape the industry's box office dynamics.

2. Socially Relevant Themes: Malayalam cinema has often been a platform for socially relevant themes, addressing issues like caste, gender, political struggles, and personal identity.

Over the years, Malayalam cinema has received international acclaim, particularly for its realism and cinematic innovation.

Conclusion

Malayalam cinema, from its humble beginnings to its current status, has consistently delivered films that combine artistic value and commercial appeal. Whether through the Golden Age, the New Wave Cinema, or modern blockbusters, the industry has always stayed relevant to the times while continuing to push the boundaries of filmmaking. With its wide range of genres, experimental narratives, and powerful performances, Malayalam cinema remains a major force in Indian cinema and has earned a reputation on the international stage as well.
